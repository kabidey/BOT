# Non-Convertible Debentures (NCDs)

## Overview
Non-Convertible Debentures (NCDs) are fixed-income debt instruments issued by corporates to raise capital from the public. Unlike convertible debentures, NCDs cannot be converted into equity shares of the issuing company. **Mackertich ONE**, the wealth-management vertical of SMIFS Ltd, assists eligible investors in evaluating, subscribing to, and managing NCD allocations across primary and secondary markets.

## Coupon Types
NCDs typically offer one of three coupon structures:

- **Fixed Coupon**: A pre-determined interest rate paid periodically (monthly, quarterly, semi-annually, or annually) over the tenure. Most common for retail-focused issues.
- **Floating Coupon**: Interest is benchmarked to a reference rate (e.g., G-Sec yield + spread) and reset periodically.
- **Cumulative**: Interest accrues and is paid as a lump sum on maturity along with the principal.

The coupon rate offered depends on the issuer's credit rating, tenure, and prevailing market yields. Investment-grade issuers (AAA / AA+) typically offer 8.5%–10.5%, while AA/A-rated issuers may offer 10.5%–12.5%.

## Secured vs Unsecured NCDs
- **Secured NCDs** are backed by a charge on identified assets of the issuer — receivables, fixed assets, or hypothecation of book debts. In the event of issuer default, secured holders have priority claim. SMIFS strongly recommends secured NCDs for first-time fixed-income investors.
- **Unsecured NCDs** rank with general unsecured creditors and offer higher coupons to compensate for the absence of collateral. Suitable only for investors who can absorb credit risk and who diversify across multiple issuers.

## Taxation
NCDs are taxed under two heads in India:

- **Interest income**: Added to the investor's total income and taxed at the applicable slab rate. There is no concessional rate for interest from listed NCDs.
- **Capital gains on sale before maturity**:
  - Listed NCDs held for more than 12 months: Long-term capital gains at 10% without indexation.
  - Listed NCDs held for 12 months or less: Short-term capital gains at slab rate.
  - Unlisted NCDs held for more than 36 months: Long-term capital gains at 20% with indexation.

TDS is generally not deducted on listed NCDs held in demat form, but it is the investor's responsibility to declare interest income.

## Retail vs HNI Allocation
SEBI mandates issuers to reserve a minimum of 40% of the public issue for the retail category (individual applicants subscribing up to ₹10 lakhs). HNI investors (applications above ₹10 lakhs) typically receive their own reserved bucket of 30%, with the remainder allocated to QIBs and corporates. SMIFS advises HNIs to apply through the ASBA mechanism and to monitor cut-off dates as oversubscribed issues are allotted on a proportionate basis.

## Risk Considerations
NCDs are not risk-free. The principal risks are credit risk (issuer default), interest rate risk (price erosion if rates rise), and liquidity risk (limited secondary market depth for many issues). SMIFS recommends restricting NCD exposure to no more than 30% of an investor's total fixed-income allocation and diversifying across at least four issuers and rating buckets.
